C-PTSD & the Immigrant Family Experience

For those who grew up in families where love was expressed through achievement, where your worth felt conditional on how well you performed, how little you needed, and how seamlessly you translated a world your parents couldn't fully navigate.

C-PTSD Parentified Child Immigrant Families Emotional Neglect Identity & Belonging Intergenerational Trauma

Growing up as the child of immigrants often means carrying weight that was never yours to hold. You may have served as a language broker, cultural interpreter, or emotional anchor for your parents, all before you had the language to name your own needs.

In families where survival was the primary focus, emotional attunement wasn't always available. Love arrived through sacrifice and provision, not through being truly seen. Over time, this shapes how you see yourself: someone who earns connection, who performs their worth, who doesn't quite know how to simply exist.

Complex PTSD from this kind of chronic relational environment can look like perfectionism, people-pleasing, difficulty trusting your own emotions, or a persistent sense of not belonging anywhere, not fully here, not fully there. Therapy offers a space to grieve what wasn't given, understand what was inherited, and slowly build a relationship with yourself that isn't conditional.

Somatic IFS & Parts Work

For those who sense different parts of themselves in conflict: the part that wants to rest and the part that never stops pushing; the part that longs for closeness and the part that always pulls away.

Parts Work Self Leadership Inner Critic Protectors & Exiles Nervous System Embodied Healing

Internal Family Systems understands the psyche as made up of many parts, each with its own perspective, its own history, and its own role it learned to play to keep you safe. The part that criticizes you harshly. The part that shuts down when things get too close. The part that still carries the pain of something that happened long ago. None of these parts are your enemy. They are trying to protect something tender.

Somatic IFS adds the dimension of the body to this work. We don't just think about your parts; we notice where they live in you. A tightness in the chest. A heaviness in the shoulders. A held breath. The body holds what the mind sometimes can't articulate, and learning to listen to it is one of the most profound forms of self-knowledge there is.

This work invites the deepest part of you, what IFS calls the Self, to lead. Not by suppressing or managing your parts, but by turning toward them with genuine curiosity and care. Over time, this shifts the entire inner landscape: less internal war, more spaciousness. Less performing, more simply being.

My Approach

How I work

I hold as fundamental that every person deserves to feel genuinely met. My approach to psychotherapy is rooted in presence: the belief that healing unfolds in the quality of the space between therapist and client. I practice with a deep attunement to the body, drawing on somatic awareness to help clients reconnect with the wisdom held in their nervous systems, not just their narratives.

Alongside this, I bring a profound commitment to deep listening, not simply hearing words, but attending to the subtle rhythms of affect, silence, and sensation that often carry what language cannot. I approach each person with the conviction that being truly seen and accepted is itself transformative; I strive to meet each client with warmth, curiosity, and an unwavering sense of their inherent worth.

I also hold cultural context as an essential lens, recognizing that our histories, identities, and communities shape how we experience ourselves, our pain, and what healing can look like. Whether working across language, culture, or lived experience, I create a therapeutic relationship that feels genuinely safe, attuned, and affirming of the whole person.

EMDR Therapy

Healing the nervous system
at its roots

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) is a well-researched, evidence-based therapy that helps the brain complete its natural processing of traumatic memories that have become "stuck" and continue to cause distress.

"EMDR therapy doesn't require you to talk in detail about a distressing event; it works with the body's own healing mechanisms."

— EMDR International Association
What does EMDR treat?
EMDR is effective for PTSD, complex trauma, anxiety, phobias, grief, performance anxiety, and more. It is recognized by the APA, WHO, and VA as a first-line treatment for trauma.
What does a session feel like?
Sessions begin with establishing safety and understanding your history. During processing, you'll hold a distressing memory in mind while engaging in bilateral stimulation (eye movements or tapping). Many people describe sessions as surprisingly gentle.
How many sessions will I need?
Single-incident traumas often resolve in 8–12 sessions. Complex or developmental trauma may take longer. We'll move at a pace that feels safe and supportive for you.
Do I have to talk about the trauma in detail?
No. EMDR is unique in that it does not require detailed verbal processing of the traumatic event. This makes it accessible for people who find talk therapy re-traumatizing.
Is EMDR only for trauma?
Not at all. EMDR can be used for anxiety, depression, negative self-beliefs, grief, and any experience that left an emotional imprint. It works wherever the nervous system is holding distress.
