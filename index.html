C-PTSD & the Immigrant Family Experience

For those who grew up in families where love was expressed through achievement — where your worth felt conditional on how well you performed, how little you needed, and how seamlessly you translated a world your parents couldn't fully navigate.

C-PTSD Parentified Child Immigrant Families Emotional Neglect Identity & Belonging Intergenerational Trauma

Growing up as the child of immigrants often means carrying weight that was never yours to hold. You may have served as a language broker, cultural interpreter, or emotional anchor for your parents — all before you had the language to name your own needs.

In families where survival was the primary focus, emotional attunement wasn't always available. Love arrived through sacrifice and provision, not through being truly seen. Over time, this shapes how you see yourself: someone who earns connection, who performs their worth, who doesn't quite know how to simply exist.

Complex PTSD from this kind of chronic relational environment can look like perfectionism, people-pleasing, difficulty trusting your own emotions, or a persistent sense of not belonging anywhere — not fully here, not fully there. Therapy offers a space to grieve what wasn't given, understand what was inherited, and slowly build a relationship with yourself that isn't conditional.

🧠
Neurodivergent Adults

For those who have spent a lifetime adapting to a world that wasn't built for the way they think, feel, and experience — and are ready to stop apologizing for it.

ADHD Autism Twice Exceptional Sensory Processing Identity & Self-Acceptance Burnout

Many neurodivergent clients describe their experience as trying to fit a square peg into a round hole. Adapting to a predominantly neurotypical world can take enormous effort — and over time, that effort accumulates. Chronic stress, self-doubt, and a sense of being fundamentally different are not character flaws. They are the natural result of navigating a culture not designed with you in mind.

I hold a non-pathologizing, deeply respectful stance toward neurodivergence. My work supports clients in building clarity, self-trust, and sustainable ways of relating — to themselves and to others. I stay curious about each person's unique experience, because while certain traits may form recognizable patterns, every individual's relationship with their own neurodivergence is distinct.

I integrate somatic approaches into this work, recognizing that language can sometimes be limiting. The body and felt sense often offer a more grounded way to track experience — particularly for clients who process at a high cognitive level but struggle to access or articulate their inner world. My goal is never to help you fit in more. It's to help you understand yourself more fully, and decide for yourself how you want to move through the world.

EMDR Therapy

Healing the nervous system
at its roots

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) is a well-researched, evidence-based therapy that helps the brain complete its natural processing of traumatic memories — memories that have become "stuck" and continue to cause distress.

"EMDR therapy doesn't require you to talk in detail about a distressing event — it works with the body's own healing mechanisms."

— EMDR International Association
What does EMDR treat?
EMDR is effective for PTSD, complex trauma, anxiety, phobias, grief, performance anxiety, and more. It is recognized by the APA, WHO, and VA as a first-line treatment for trauma.
What does a session feel like?
Sessions begin with establishing safety and understanding your history. During processing, you'll hold a distressing memory in mind while engaging in bilateral stimulation (eye movements or tapping). Many people describe sessions as surprisingly gentle.
How many sessions will I need?
Single-incident traumas often resolve in 8–12 sessions. Complex or developmental trauma may take longer. We'll move at a pace that feels safe and supportive for you.
Do I have to talk about the trauma in detail?
No. EMDR is unique in that it does not require detailed verbal processing of the traumatic event. This makes it accessible for people who find talk therapy re-traumatizing.
Is EMDR only for trauma?
Not at all. EMDR can be used for anxiety, depression, negative self-beliefs, grief, and any experience that left an emotional imprint. It works wherever the nervous system is holding distress.
